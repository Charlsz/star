# Galaxies: Concepts and Morphology

## 1. Definition and global properties

A galaxy is a gravitationally bound system of stars, gas, dust, dark matter, and often a central supermassive black hole, typically spanning from a few thousand to hundreds of thousands of light-years in size. [science.nasa](https://science.nasa.gov/universe/galaxies/types/)
Galaxies contain from millions to trillions of stars plus interstellar gas and dust, embedded in a much larger dark-matter halo that dominates the mass budget. [imagine.gsfc.nasa](https://imagine.gsfc.nasa.gov/science/objects/galaxies1.html)
Galaxies are broadly divided by large-scale appearance (morphology) into spirals, ellipticals, lenticulars, and irregular systems, with morphology correlating with physical properties such as gas content and star-formation activity. [en.wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_morphological_classification)

## 2. Classical morphology schemes

### 2.1 Hubble sequence

The Hubble sequence (Hubble tuning-fork diagram) is the classical visual classification scheme organizing galaxies into ellipticals (E), lenticulars (S0), normal spirals (Sa–Sc), barred spirals (SBa–SBc), and irregulars (Irr). [pages.astronomy.ua](https://pages.astronomy.ua.edu/keel/galaxies/classify.html)
Ellipticals occupy one branch, with subtypes E0–E7 indicating increasing apparent ellipticity, while lenticulars (S0) sit between ellipticals and spirals, possessing a disk plus bulge but lacking prominent spiral arms. [ned.ipac.caltech](https://ned.ipac.caltech.edu/level5/Sept02/Buta/paper.pdf)
Normal and barred spirals form two “fork” branches, where subtypes (Sa/SBa to Sc/SBc) encode bulge-to-disk ratio and tightness of spiral arms, and irregular galaxies lie outside this orderly sequence. [schoolsobservatory](https://www.schoolsobservatory.org/learn/space/galaxies/classification)

### 2.2 de Vaucouleurs and extended schemes

The de Vaucouleurs system extends the Hubble sequence with continuous parameters and additional symbols to represent features such as rings, lenses, and bars. [en.wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_morphological_classification)
More recent schemes include quantitative indices (e.g., concentration, asymmetry, clumpiness) and multi-parameter classifications that allow better comparison between visual inspection and automated classifications. [phas.ubc](https://phas.ubc.ca/~hickson/astr505/astr505_2016-2.pdf)

## 3. Main galaxy types

### 3.1 Spiral galaxies

Spiral galaxies consist of a central bulge, a rotating disk with spiral arms, and an extended halo of stars and dark matter. [science.nasa](https://science.nasa.gov/universe/galaxies/types/)
The disk typically contains substantial cold gas and dust and hosts ongoing star formation, making spiral arms appear bright and clumpy due to young, massive stars and H II regions. [imagine.gsfc.nasa](https://imagine.gsfc.nasa.gov/science/objects/galaxies1.html)
Subtypes Sa–Sc indicate decreasing bulge dominance and more open spiral arms: Sa systems have large bulges and tightly wound arms, while Sc systems have small bulges and loosely wound arms. [pages.astronomy.ua](https://pages.astronomy.ua.edu/keel/galaxies/classify.html)

Reference:  
- NASA spiral description: <https://science.nasa.gov/universe/galaxies/types/> [science.nasa](https://science.nasa.gov/universe/galaxies/types/)

### 3.2 Barred spiral galaxies

Barred spirals (SBa–SBc) exhibit a linear bar feature across the central region, from which spiral arms emerge. [britannica](https://www.britannica.com/science/galaxy/Types-of-galaxies)
The bar redistributes angular momentum and gas, often funneling material inward and influencing central star formation and nuclear activity. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.09610)

Reference:  
- Morphology overview including bars: <https://ned.ipac.caltech.edu/level5/Sept02/Buta/paper.pdf> [ned.ipac.caltech](https://ned.ipac.caltech.edu/level5/Sept02/Buta/paper.pdf)

### 3.3 Elliptical galaxies

Elliptical galaxies show smooth, featureless light profiles with approximately ellipsoidal shapes and little or no visible disk or spiral structure. [britannica](https://www.britannica.com/science/galaxy/Types-of-galaxies)
They are typically gas-poor and dominated by older stellar populations, with low present-day star-formation rates compared to spirals. [imagine.gsfc.nasa](https://imagine.gsfc.nasa.gov/science/objects/galaxies1.html)
Ellipticals are labeled E0–E7 based on projected flattening, with E0 nearly round and E7 highly elongated. [britannica](https://www.britannica.com/science/galaxy/Types-of-galaxies)

References:  
- Britannica galaxy types overview: <https://www.britannica.com/science/galaxy/Types-of-galaxies> [britannica](https://www.britannica.com/science/galaxy/Types-of-galaxies)
- NASA galaxy types page: <https://science.nasa.gov/universe/galaxies/types/> [science.nasa](https://science.nasa.gov/universe/galaxies/types/)

### 3.4 Lenticular galaxies (S0)

Lenticular galaxies (S0) bridge ellipticals and spirals, possessing a prominent disk and bulge but lacking clear spiral arms and strong ongoing star formation. [en.wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_morphological_classification)
They often show a smooth disk, limited dust, and properties suggesting they may be former spirals that lost their gas, for example via environmental processes. [phas.ubc](https://phas.ubc.ca/~hickson/astr505/astr505_2016-2.pdf)

### 3.5 Irregular and peculiar galaxies

Irregular galaxies have no obvious regular shape, often showing chaotic, clumpy structures without a dominant bulge or well-defined disk. [imagine.gsfc.nasa](https://imagine.gsfc.nasa.gov/science/objects/galaxies1.html)
Many irregular and peculiar galaxies are interacting or merging systems, where tidal forces and collisions distort morphology beyond simple Hubble-sequence categories. [ned.ipac.caltech](https://ned.ipac.caltech.edu/level5/Sept02/Buta/paper.pdf)

## 4. Image-based morphology features

For automated image classification, the following visual and quantitative features are central:

- **Bulge-to-disk ratio**: relative prominence of the central bulge versus the disk, distinguishing ellipticals and early-type spirals from late-type spirals. [phas.ubc](https://phas.ubc.ca/~hickson/astr505/astr505_2016-2.pdf)
- **Spiral arms**: presence, number, and tightness of spiral arms separating spirals from smooth systems and distinguishing spiral subtypes. [pages.astronomy.ua](https://pages.astronomy.ua.edu/keel/galaxies/classify.html)
- **Bars**: elongated central bars, including length and prominence, used to identify barred spirals. [en.wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_morphological_classification)
- **Asymmetry**: degree of rotational symmetry; mergers and irregulars tend to show strong asymmetries. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.09610)
- **Clumpiness**: patchy, high-frequency structure tracing regions of active star formation in disks and irregulars. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.09610)
- **Inclination**: apparent flattening due to viewing angle; edge-on spirals may appear similar to ellipticals in simple projections. [ned.ipac.caltech](https://ned.ipac.caltech.edu/level5/Sept02/Buta/paper.pdf)
- **Rings and lenses**: ring features and lens-like components that refine classification within spirals and lenticulars. [kudzu.astr.ua](https://kudzu.astr.ua.edu/psss/galaxy_morphology.pdf)

Reference for quantitative indices and examples:  
- Galaxy morphology (quantitative indices): <https://kudzu.astr.ua.edu/psss/galaxy_morphology.pdf> [kudzu.astr.ua](https://kudzu.astr.ua.edu/psss/galaxy_morphology.pdf)

## 5. Physical interpretation of morphology

Galaxy morphology correlates with star-formation activity, gas content, and environment. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.09610)
Spiral galaxies typically contain more cold gas and dust and show active star formation, while ellipticals are more gas-poor and dominated by older stars. [science.nasa](https://science.nasa.gov/universe/galaxies/types/)
Dense environments such as galaxy clusters often host a higher fraction of early-type galaxies (ellipticals and S0), consistent with environmental effects like tidal interactions and gas stripping. [ned.ipac.caltech](https://ned.ipac.caltech.edu/level5/Sept02/Buta/paper.pdf)
Mergers and strong interactions can transform spirals into disturbed or early-type systems, leaving signatures such as tidal tails and shells and altering morphology over time. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.09610)

- NASA galaxy overview: <https://imagine.gsfc.nasa.gov/science/objects/galaxies1.html> [imagine.gsfc.nasa](https://imagine.gsfc.nasa.gov/science/objects/galaxies1.html)
- Morphological classification in context of evolution: <https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.09610> [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.09610)

## 6. Morphology labels relevant for STAR

For machine-learning classification, simplified label sets are commonly used:

- **Binary labels**: e.g., spiral vs elliptical. [spacetelescope.github](https://spacetelescope.github.io/mast_notebooks/notebooks/SDSS/SDSS_LEGACY_IMAGING_galaxy_morphology/galaxy_morphology.html)
- **Three-class sets**: spiral vs elliptical vs irregular. [schoolsobservatory](https://www.schoolsobservatory.org/learn/space/galaxies/classification)
- **Galaxy Zoo–style labels**: “smooth” vs “features or disk” vs “star or artifact,” plus more detailed answers about bars, edge-on orientation, and spiral arm characteristics. [zooniverse](https://www.zooniverse.org/projects/zookeeper/galaxy-zoo)
- **Early-/late-type labels**: early type (elliptical and lenticular) versus late type (spiral and irregular) as a coarse division. [academic.oup](https://academic.oup.com/mnras/article/512/2/2222/6546172)

These label sets trade physical detail for robustness and ease of use in large-scale surveys and ML pipelines. [academic.oup](https://academic.oup.com/mnras/article/512/2/2222/6546172)

## 7. Morphology datasets and catalogs

### 7.1 Galaxy Zoo

Galaxy Zoo is a large citizen-science project where volunteers visually classified galaxies from surveys such as the Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS). [zooniverse](https://www.zooniverse.org/projects/zookeeper/galaxy-zoo)
The project provides public catalogs with vote fractions for morphological questions (e.g., “smooth,” “features,” “edge-on,” “barred”), which can be converted into probabilistic or hard labels for training ML models. [data.galaxyzoo](https://data.galaxyzoo.org)
Galaxy Zoo data have been widely used to train automated classifiers and to study the consistency and biases of human and machine classifications. [zooniverse](https://www.zooniverse.org/projects/zookeeper/galaxy-zoo)

Links:  
- Project: <https://www.zooniverse.org/projects/zookeeper/galaxy-zoo> [zooniverse](https://www.zooniverse.org/projects/zookeeper/galaxy-zoo)

### 7.2 SDSS imaging and value-added catalogs

The Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS) provides multi-band imaging and spectroscopy of millions of galaxies, forming the basis of many morphology catalogs. [sdss](https://www.sdss.org/dr18/data_access/value-added-catalogs/)
Value-added catalogs combine SDSS photometry and spectroscopy with derived quantities, including morphology classifications from visual inspection or automatic algorithms. [sdss](https://www.sdss.org/dr18/data_access/value-added-catalogs/)
SDSS documentation includes algorithms and flags related to morphological classification and object type, which help construct clean training sets. [sdss4](https://www.sdss4.org/dr17/algorithms/classify/)

Links:

### 7.3 Large morphology catalogs

Automated systems based on image-analysis and machine-learning methods have produced morphological classifications for millions of galaxies. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/1602.06854)

Other projects, such as MaNGA visual morphology catalogs, offer detailed classifications for smaller but information-rich samples with integral-field spectroscopy. [academic.oup](https://academic.oup.com/mnras/article/512/2/2222/6546172)

## 8. Machine-learning context for galaxy morphology

Modern work on galaxy morphology increasingly employs supervised and unsupervised ML methods, using labeled datasets such as Galaxy Zoo and SDSS-derived catalogs. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/1602.06854)
Typical supervised tasks include predicting discrete morphology classes (e.g., spiral vs elliptical vs irregular) or regressing vote fractions that encode probabilistic classifications. [academic.oup](https://academic.oup.com/mnras/article/512/2/2222/6546172)
Deep learning models, especially convolutional neural networks (CNNs), are widely used to learn features directly from multi-band images, often outperforming traditional hand-crafted feature methods on large datasets. [spacetelescope.github](https://spacetelescope.github.io/mast_notebooks/notebooks/SDSS/SDSS_LEGACY_IMAGING_galaxy_morphology/galaxy_morphology.html)
Recent reviews emphasize the importance of uncertainty quantification, class-imbalance handling, and careful validation when applying ML to morphological classification. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.09610)

Reference:  
- Morphological classification review: <https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.09610> [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.09610)

## 9. Typical challenges and edge cases

Galaxy morphology classification faces several systematic challenges that are relevant for any image-based system:

- **Low signal-to-noise and resolution**: faint or distant galaxies may lack clear structural features, leading to ambiguous labels and reduced classification accuracy. [phas.ubc](https://phas.ubc.ca/~hickson/astr505/astr505_2016-2.pdf)
- **Projection and inclination**: edge-on spirals can resemble ellipticals, and small, face-on disks may appear smooth, complicating automated separation. [ned.ipac.caltech](https://ned.ipac.caltech.edu/level5/Sept02/Buta/paper.pdf)
- **Mergers and interactions**: strongly interacting or merging systems often display irregular, asymmetric shapes that do not fit simple labels. [ned.ipac.caltech](https://ned.ipac.caltech.edu/level5/Sept02/Buta/paper.pdf)
- **Label noise and disagreement**: human classifiers (including citizen scientists) can disagree on difficult cases, and vote fractions must be translated into labels carefully. [data.galaxyzoo](https://data.galaxyzoo.org)
- **Class imbalance**: some types (e.g., strongly barred spirals, rare peculiar systems) are underrepresented, influencing training and evaluation. [academic.oup](https://academic.oup.com/mnras/article/512/2/2222/6546172)

These effects are important both scientifically and for the design of robust machine-learning models and evaluation metrics. [phas.ubc](https://phas.ubc.ca/~hickson/astr505/astr505_2016-2.pdf)

## 10. Summary of morphology information relevant to STAR

For a galaxy-focused module in a taxonomy and recognition platform:

- Galaxies are classified primarily by large-scale structure into ellipticals, lenticulars, spirals (normal and barred), and irregulars, following schemes such as the Hubble sequence. [science.nasa](https://science.nasa.gov/universe/galaxies/types/)
- Image-based morphology uses visual and quantitative features such as bulge prominence, spiral arms, bars, asymmetry, clumpiness, and inclination. [kudzu.astr.ua](https://kudzu.astr.ua.edu/psss/galaxy_morphology.pdf)
- Morphology correlates with physical properties including gas content, star-formation rate, stellar populations, and environmental history, making classification scientifically meaningful beyond appearance alone. [science.nasa](https://science.nasa.gov/universe/galaxies/types/)
- Public datasets (Galaxy Zoo, SDSS imaging, and large morphology catalogs) provide labeled galaxies suitable for supervised ML, including both discrete labels and probabilistic vote fractions. [sdss](https://www.sdss.org/dr18/data_access/value-added-catalogs/)
- Common ML label sets simplify the full taxonomy into binary or few-class problems (e.g., spiral vs elliptical vs irregular or smooth vs featured vs artifact), which are well-suited for initial classification pipelines. [zooniverse](https://www.zooniverse.org/projects/zookeeper/galaxy-zoo)
- Challenges such as noise, projection effects, mergers, and label ambiguity must be considered when designing models, datasets, and evaluation procedures. [phas.ubc](https://phas.ubc.ca/~hickson/astr505/astr505_2016-2.pdf)
